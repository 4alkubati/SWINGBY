### Post 2 — Founder personal — Why I built this

Three years ago, I booked a handyman off an online classifieds site. He didn't show up. I found another one. He showed up 4 hours late and charged me more than the quote.

I spent the next month asking around. Turns out almost everyone in Calgary had a version of this story. The problem isn't a lack of service businesses — Calgary has thousands of them. The problem is trust. There's no reliable way to know who to hire, and no protection if something goes wrong.

SwingBy is my answer to that problem. We vet every business that joins. We hold the payment on the platform and release it in two stages — half at booking, half when the job is done. We give clients real reviews from real booked jobs.

We launched this week. 40 businesses already on the platform. Come see what we're building.

### Post 3 — Founder personal — What I learned in week 1

Week 1 update: here's what actually happened.

We expected our first challenge to be getting clients to try a new platform. It wasn't — once people understood the concept, adoption was quick. The harder challenge was the middle step: convincing clients that the reviews were real and the businesses were actually vetted. That trust gap takes time to close.

Three things that worked: word of mouth from the first 15 businesses we signed, a clear explanation of how escrow works, and responding to every DM personally.

Three things that didn't: our initial onboarding flow was too long (we cut it by 60%), we sent too many notifications in the first 48 hours, and we underestimated how long business verification takes.

Building is mostly figuring out what doesn't work yet.

### Post 4 — Company page — Escrow explainer

Most home service transactions in Calgary still happen in cash.

No contract. No receipt. No protection if the job isn't done right, or isn't done at all.

SwingBy uses escrow for every booking. Payment is held when booking is confirmed. Released when the client marks the job complete. If there's a dispute, we review and resolve it.

This isn't a new concept in marketplace design. But for home services specifically, it's rare. The businesses on our platform actually prefer it — because it means they always get paid for jobs they complete.

Fair for clients. Fair for businesses. That's the point.

### Post 6 — Company page — Platform trust

A question we get often: "How do you know the businesses on your platform are legit?"

Here's the short answer: we check.

Every business that applies to join SwingBy goes through a review process: business registration verification, license check (where applicable for regulated trades), ID verification, and reference review.

After they're on the platform, clients do the rest. Every review on SwingBy is tied to a confirmed booking. There's no way to purchase or fake a review. A business with 40 five-star reviews earned every one of them.

We've declined roughly 30% of applicants since launch. That's not a number we're proud of — it's a number we think is necessary.

### Post 7 — Founder personal — Marketplace dynamics (build-in-public)

Building a two-sided marketplace is a chicken-and-egg problem that never fully goes away.

You need businesses to attract clients. You need clients to attract businesses. Early on, you solve this by hand — you recruit both sides, curate aggressively, and accept that your unit economics won't look good for a while.

Three weeks in, we have 180 businesses and 900 active clients in Calgary. The ratio I'm watching: qualified businesses per category per neighbourhood. If a client in Kensington posts a cleaning job and only gets one response, we have a supply problem. Right now, they're getting 4–6. That's the number I care about.

Marketplace health isn't about total size. It's about depth where demand is.

## Week 3 — Business Side & Growth

### Post 9 — Founder personal — On pricing transparency

We publish our fees publicly: 10% platform cut, taken from the second half of the business's payment.

This is deliberate. A business quoting $200 on SwingBy knows exactly what they'll net. A client paying $200 knows exactly where it goes. No hidden fees at checkout. No confusion at payout.

Marketplace transparency is unusual. Most platforms obscure their fees because revealing them invites scrutiny. We think the opposite: if you can't defend your fee structure out loud, something's wrong with it.

10% is the standard for two-sided marketplaces at our scale. As volume grows and our costs come down, that number can go down too. That's a public commitment.

### Post 11 — Founder personal — What we won't build

We get asked regularly about adding features: a messaging tool pre-booking, a bidding system, a subscription plan for businesses.

Here's what we've decided not to build, and why.

Pre-booking messaging: we tried it. It just moved the chaos earlier in the process. Clients got bombarded. Businesses wasted time on bad leads. We removed it.

Bidding wars: the evidence from other platforms is clear — race-to-the-bottom pricing destroys quality. The businesses that win on price alone aren't the ones clients want to hire. We want reviews to win, not bids.

Business subscriptions: you shouldn't pay us to be on the platform. You pay when you earn. That's the right incentive structure.

Building less, on purpose, is hard. But the simplicity is the product.

## Weeks 3–4 Continued — Business Development & Momentum

### Post 17 — Founder personal — The two-sided problem solved

Most marketplaces fail because they solve for one side too early.

Build for clients first and you get frustrated businesses who feel like they're on a platform that ignores them. Build for businesses first and you get frustrated clients who don't trust the platform.

The only way to get this right is to solve both simultaneously, which is expensive and slow. You have to recruit each side by hand. You have to be present for the early transactions. You have to adjust quickly when either side gives you feedback.

We've done all three. It's unglamorous work. But 1,200+ successful bookings in 30 days suggests it's working.

### Post 19 — Founder personal — Why escrow changes the dynamic

Before SwingBy, the typical home service transaction in Calgary worked like this: client finds business, client pays cash, hopes for the best.

This isn't a software insight. It's a human one.
