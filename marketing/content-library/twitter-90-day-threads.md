### Thread 2 — Marketplace dynamics (founder insights)

1/ Building a two-sided marketplace is a chicken-and-egg problem that never fully goes away.

A thread on how we solved it in Calgary. 🧵

2/ You need businesses to attract clients. You need clients to attract businesses.

Early on, you solve this by hand. You recruit both sides manually. You curate aggressively. You accept bad unit economics for months.

3/ The metric that matters isn't total platform size. It's depth.

In Calgary, if a client in Kensington posts a cleaning job, do they get 4+ competitive quotes? Or just 1?

We optimize for the first outcome. Even if it means slow total growth.

4/ What worked for us: recruiting businesses by category and neighbourhood, not by blasting the whole city.

Fill the inner-city first. Then ring suburbs. Don't try to serve all of Calgary Day 1.

5/ Supply and demand in a local marketplace are hyperlocal. A business 30km away is nearly useless to a client who needs someone today.

You have to build neighbourhood by neighbourhood.

6/ Three months in: 180 businesses, spread across 15 Calgary neighbourhoods. Average of 4.8 business responses per client job post.

That's the number. Not total businesses. Responses per post.

7/ If you're building a marketplace: track depth, not breadth. The growth metrics that get you investor attention (total GMV, total users) can hide a product that's thin and fragile.

Know where it's working, not just how big it is.

### Thread 3 — Escrow and trust

1/ The home services industry has a payment problem. Here's how SwingBy fixes it. 🧵

2/ Most home service transactions still happen in cash. No contract. No receipt. No protection.

The business gets paid before the job is complete. The client has no recourse if something goes wrong.

3/ We use escrow for every booking.

Client confirms booking → payment held
Job completed → payment released
No-show or dispute → payment reviewed

4/ This changes the incentive structure for everyone.

Businesses show up because they'll get paid. Clients don't stress because their money is safe. Disputes are rare because both parties know the rules.

5/ Our no-show rate: 0.4%. Our dispute rate: 2.9%.

Are those the result of escrow? Partly. Also: vetting, reviews, and the fact that both sides have real skin in the game.

6/ The businesses on SwingBy actually prefer escrow. They used to chase invoices. Now they get paid the day the job is confirmed (50%) and the day it's complete (50%).

More predictable than cash.

7/ Platform trust isn't just about features. It's about structure. Build the right incentives and most people will behave well most of the time.

### Thread 4 — How to hire a local service in Calgary

1/ How to hire any home service in Calgary without getting burned. 🧵

2/ Step 1: Know what fair price looks like before you search.

House cleaning: $80–$200 depending on size
Handyman: $50–$90/hour
Dog walking: $18–$40/60 min
Moving: varies heavily by size/distance

If a quote is 50% below these ranges — ask why.

3/ Step 2: Check reviews before you respond.

Not just the star rating. Read the actual reviews. Look for specifics: "showed up on time," "did exactly what was quoted," "treated the home with respect."

Generic five-stars tell you nothing.

4/ Step 3: Confirm scope in writing before booking.

"Deep clean, 3-bed, includes oven and fridge" is a booking. "A cleaning" is a dispute waiting to happen.

5/ Step 4: Pay through a platform that protects you.

Cash = no recourse.
Credit card on Kijiji = limited recourse.
SwingBy escrow = full protection until job complete.

6/ Step 5: Leave a review after.

Good reviews help other clients find trustworthy businesses. Bad reviews (with specifics) help businesses improve.

The platform only works if people review.

7/ That's it. 5 steps. Most people skip all five and then wonder how they ended up with a no-show.

Build good habits. Hire well. swingbyy.com

### Thread 5 — Build-in-public: Month 1

1/ One month building SwingBy in public. Here's what I got wrong. 🧵

2/ The onboarding flow was too long. We had 8 screens. Should have been 3. We cut it and conversion went up 40%.

3/ We sent too many notifications in week 1. Clients muted us. Businesses said they were overwhelmed. We cut frequency by 60% and open rates went up.

4/ We built the pre-booking messaging feature too early. Nobody used it. Businesses found it added noise without converting to bookings. We removed it.

5/ The support queue was bigger than expected. Every platform issue that's "minor" to us is "major" to the client experiencing it. We needed to staff support faster.

6/ What worked: being personal. I responded to every negative review personally in month 1. Not with corporate templates. With real responses. It converted complaints into advocates.

7/ What surprised me most: how much goodwill clients have for a new local platform. They want this to exist. They'll give you more grace than you deserve if you're honest and responsive.

8/ Month 2 starts now. The errors are different but the approach is the same: build fast, admit mistakes, fix quickly, be honest publicly.

### Thread 7 — Why we stayed Calgary-only

1/ We could have launched in 5 cities. We launched in 1. Here's why that was the right call. 🧵

2/ Every marketplace that expanded too fast ended up with thin supply in too many markets.

Clients in City B have a bad experience because there aren't enough businesses. Businesses in City C leave because there aren't enough clients.

3/ The right way to build a local marketplace: go deep in one city until demand is saturated. Then expand.

"Deep" means: client in any inner-city neighbourhood can post a job and get 5+ competitive quotes within an hour.

4/ We're not there yet. Almost — but not in every category, every neighbourhood.

That's the work in front of us in Calgary before we even think about the next city.

5/ It's also why we've been selective about which businesses we onboard. It's better to have 180 excellent businesses than 500 mediocre ones.

Quality depth > quantity breadth.

6/ When we expand: Edmonton, probably. Then Vancouver Island. Then Ontario.

The playbook will be the same. Start in one neighbourhood. Fill it. Expand outward.

### Thread 8 — The review problem in home services

1/ Why reviews in home services are mostly useless — and what we did about it. 🧵

2/ The problem with Google/Yelp reviews for trades and home services: you can't verify they're from real clients.

Reviews are often written by friends and family. Or by the business owner themselves. Or purchased.

3/ There's also survivorship bias. The only clients who leave reviews are the very happy and the very angry. The middle — the unremarkable good service — rarely gets documented.

4/ On SwingBy, every review is tied to a confirmed booking. You can only review a business you've hired. No workarounds.

5/ We also prompt for specific details: "What did they do well? Was the job completed as described? Would you hire them again?"

Specific prompts get specific reviews. Specific reviews convert better.

6/ The result: a review corpus that's actually informative. Not just star ratings — descriptions of what the service was like, what the business did, whether they showed up.

7/ We're not perfect at this yet. But the review system is probably the most important infrastructure in the whole platform. Getting it right matters more than almost anything else we build.

### Thread 9 — Pricing model transparency

1/ We charge 10% of the booking value. Here's the full breakdown of why that's the number. 🧵

2/ Our costs:
- Payment processing: ~2.5% of transaction value
- Support, dispute resolution: ~2–3% effective cost
- Infrastructure, hosting: ~1–2%
- Customer acquisition: ~2% (currently very low due to word-of-mouth)

That's roughly 7.5–9.5% in direct costs at current scale.

3/ The 0.5–2.5% margin is thin. On purpose. We want to grow volume, not margin, in year one.

As volume grows, payment processing costs come down (volume discounts), support gets more efficient, infrastructure amortizes better.

4/ At $5M GMV annually, a 10% fee = $500k revenue. At that scale, we can operate profitably at 10%.

5/ We've been asked: will you lower the fee?

Short answer: yes, as we get more efficient. Not today. Building trust is expensive.

6/ We'll publish annual fee reviews. If we can lower it, we will. That's a public commitment.

### Thread 10 — Managing the no-show problem

1/ Marketplace no-shows are a trust killer. Here's how we handle them at SwingBy. 🧵

2/ Current no-show rate: 0.4%. That's about 5 per 1,200 bookings.

Every single one gets reviewed personally. Not a template. A real call or message.

3/ What we learned from those 5:

- 2 were communication failures. Business had an emergency and didn't know how to cancel in the app. Process issue.
- 2 were new businesses who overbooked. Scheduling issue.
- 1 was a business that clearly shouldn't be on the platform. Removed.

4/ No-show prevention at the system level:

- Businesses get a reminder notification 2 hours before the job
- Clients can confirm the business is on the way (or not) 30 min before
- If no confirmation, we proactively check in

5/ No-show resolution:

- Client contacts us within 24 hours
- If confirmed no-show: full refund, no questions
- Business gets a formal warning. Second: probation. Third: removed.

6/ The goal isn't zero no-shows — that's unrealistic. The goal is fast resolution and a platform that gets better, not worse, over time.
